# Board Template Draft

This is the first default layout for a product-grade Studio World board.

The goal is simple. A creator should understand the world structure in one scan, find dense usable information when browsing deeper, and still feel a pull toward imagining scenes, tensions and new stories.

## Core layout

The board should read from top to bottom in seven bands.

Band 1 is world identity. It contains the world title, overview and a short prologue.

Band 2 is the hero band. It contains one strong KV or world-level visual anchor plus a compact world statement.

Band 3 is the core character band.

Band 4 is the major location band.

Band 5 is the powers band. This is where organizations, factions, systems, artifacts and world rules live.

Band 6 is the motion band. This is where events, eras, conflicts, secrets and historical turning points live.

Band 7 is grouped coverage. This is where long-tail grouped characters, grouped locations and other overflow representation can sit without polluting the premium reading path.

## Default reading order

The viewer should learn the world in this order:

what this world is -> what it feels like -> who matters -> where it happens -> what governs it -> what happened and what tension remains -> what long-tail density still exists beyond the core layer

If a board does not support this order, it is probably overpacked or badly zoned.

The board should also stay legible at two zoom levels.

At far view, the reader should already see the world title, hero visual, and major band names.

At close view, the reader should be able to browse dense cards without losing the sense of which zone they are in.

## Placement rules

World title should sit alone at the top and should not compete with atom cards.

World overview and prologue should sit directly under the title and behave like a short framing panel rather than a dump of setup text.

The hero band should not try to teach the whole world. Its job is to set the world promise, the scale and the emotional weather.

The first viewport should remain overview-first. Dense support coverage can continue deeper in the board, but the opening frame should not collapse into a wall of small cards.

Each major band should have one section title and one short section body.

The section hierarchy should behave like exhibition text hierarchy:

- world-level introduction
- section-level context
- atom-level cards

This means overview panels and section panels act like orientation labels. They frame what the reader is entering and why it matters. They do not repeat metadata and they do not read like prompts.

Atom cards inside a band should be arranged in clean rows or clusters with visible gutters. No overlapping stacks for the default browsing view.

Use large zones or sections for navigation. A large board should still be understandable as a map of content, not just a pile of assets.

Work cards should not be mixed randomly into atom rows. They should either sit in the hero visual area or at the right edge of the band they belong to.

## Character band

Lead characters come first. Supporting figures come after them. Group atoms, if used for cap handling, should be visually separated from independent character cards so the board does not imply they are the same granularity.

If the character inventory is large, keep the first visible slice focused on core cast and let dense support coverage continue lower in the board instead of compressing everything into one noisy viewport.

Do not use group atoms to replace major characters. If grouping appears in the character band, it should read as overflow handling for adjacent or long-tail figures rather than as the main representation layer for the cast.

If the character band is too dense, split it into sub-clusters such as heroes, antagonists and adjacent groups rather than shrinking everything into unreadable noise.

## Location band

Location cards should be ordered from core anchors to peripheral regions.

A good order is home base, power center, frontier, hidden zone, war zone, sacred or forbidden zone.

This lets the viewer understand how the world expands outward.

## Powers band

This band should explain how the world works.

Organizations, factions, systems, artifacts and world rules need enough spacing that the user can understand institutions and mechanics separately from places and people.

## Motion band

This band should explain motion and tension.

Events, secrets, relationships and perspectives belong here because they tell the viewer what fractures the world, what keeps it moving and where new stories can start.

## Grouped coverage band

This band is a controlled fallback zone.

It exists for long-tail or overflow representation when independent treatment would overload the board or violate platform limits.

Grouped coverage should stay near the domain it belongs to, but it should still read as a secondary density layer rather than part of the premium spine.

## Density rules

Do not chase total card count on the first visible frame.

If a world grows large, use grouped overview cards and a smaller visible representative set near the top of each band, then let deeper assets continue further down the board. The first viewport should stay legible.

Repeated card types should keep consistent width, crop logic and label treatment. Density becomes readable when repetition is disciplined.

Density is not the enemy. Unshaped density is the enemy. The board should feel rich, not clogged.

## Failure conditions

The board fails if the title area is buried by atom cards.

The board fails if cards overlap so heavily that names and covers become hard to read.

The board fails if works are scattered without logic.

The board fails if the world looks like a random import result instead of a structured sample room.

The board fails if grouped coverage appears to be the main representation layer for major figures.

The board fails if hero content keeps stacking below itself until it blocks the information path.

## Next hardening step

This draft still needs exact spacing, column-count and overflow rules tied to real board dimensions. That should be validated on at least one medium world and one large world before being declared final.
