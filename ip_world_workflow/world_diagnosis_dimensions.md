# World Diagnosis Dimensions

This document defines the first version of the dimension system used before building a Neta Studio World.

Families such as mythic, political, gothic, youth-adventure or modern heroic are useful presets, but they are not the real source of truth. The reusable layer is diagnosis by dimensions. The agent should diagnose the world first, then derive coverage, writing rules, style selection and board structure from that diagnosis.

## Purpose

The dimension step exists to stop two failure modes:

- rigid templates that make every world feel the same
- free-form invention that loses source truth, hierarchy and visual identity

The goal is guide rails rather than a mold. A run should make a clear judgment about the target IP before atoms are written or board layout begins.

## Required diagnosis

Every serious world run should answer these dimensions before import planning.

### Narrative pressure

What keeps stories alive in this world?

Common sources of pressure include fate, broken order, power struggle, old secrets, coming-of-age trials, heroic identity, war exhaustion, exploration, survival threat, social belonging or daily community.

This dimension decides the core world promise and the first sentence of world metadata.

Do not diagnose by surface genre. Spaceships do not automatically mean science fiction. Armies do not automatically mean military primary. A school does not automatically mean youth-adventure. Dark imagery does not automatically mean gothic.

### Entry point

How does a creator first enter the world?

The entry point may be a protagonist, team, city, school, kingdom, battlefield, profession, open map, hidden society, institution, quest route or icon roster.

This dimension decides the first board reading path.

### Asset center of gravity

Which atom types carry the world?

Possible centers include characters, locations, organizations, objects, vehicles, creatures, powers, species, professions, historical eras, major events or relationship systems.

This dimension decides the coverage ledger and which entities must become independent atoms first.

### Relationship structure

What relationship logic defines meaning?

Common structures include inheritance, mentorship, team chemistry, faction allegiance, public identity versus private self, city ecology, sworn rivalry, forbidden attachment, family wound, institutional loyalty or professional network.

This dimension decides which relationships must appear inside card copy and which relationship or secret atoms deserve independent treatment.

### Scale

At what distance is the world normally used?

Scale may be street-level, school-level, city-level, kingdom-level, planetary, galactic, cosmic, multiverse or mixed-scale.

This dimension decides board density, section size, grouping tolerance and whether a world needs atlas-like navigation.

### Time structure

How does time organize the IP?

The world may be single-era, multi-era, cyclical, prequel-heavy, sequel-heavy, event-driven, seasonal, generational or chronicle-like.

This dimension decides whether timeline, era bands and event atoms are central or supporting material.

### Visual recognition mechanism

What makes the world recognizable at a glance?

Recognition may come from character silhouettes, costumes, uniforms, architecture, city texture, weapons, vehicles, creature design, magic effects, faction colors, material language or environmental contrast.

This dimension feeds style-space selection. The selected style must preserve the world recognition mechanism across characters, places, objects, events and key visuals.

### Prose distance

What kind of document should the visible copy feel like?

Possible distances include official databank, franchise bible, historical chronicle, character dossier, intelligence brief, haunted dossier, field guide, travel guide, team roster or tactical brief.

This dimension decides card rhythm and metadata tone. It should not override the shared rule that visible copy must be natural English and reader-facing.

### Board reading mode

What should the board feel like as a surface?

Common modes include exhibition hub, atlas desk, icon roster, city map, timeline wall, faction map, field guide, story engine board or creator workbench.

This dimension decides the board's first viewport and major zones.

## Family presets

Families are common combinations of the dimensions above. They should be treated as presets, not hard templates.

### Mythic

Typical pressure: fate, broken order, oath, inheritance, sacred geography, historical burden.

Typical entry point: prophecy bearer, fellowship, kingdom, sacred site, age transition.

Typical asset gravity: characters, locations, lineages, artifacts, events and eras.

Typical board mode: exhibition hub or epic atlas.

### Political

Typical pressure: legitimacy, coercion, institutional fracture, faction interest, state violence.

Typical entry point: regime, court, institution, faction conflict or command structure.

Typical asset gravity: organizations, leaders, institutions, locations, events and laws.

Typical board mode: faction map, intelligence brief or atlas desk.

### Gothic

Typical pressure: old wound, secret, taboo, inherited guilt, place-as-pressure.

Typical entry point: house, school, estate, city, forbidden room, family line or intimate relationship.

Typical asset gravity: locations, relationships, secrets, characters and historical scars.

Typical board mode: haunted dossier, exhibition hub or story engine board.

### Youth-Adventure

Typical pressure: first entry into a larger world, identity formation, friendship, rules, trial and discovery.

Typical entry point: protagonist, crew, school, route, hidden society or first quest.

Typical asset gravity: core cast, entry locations, rule systems, mentors, rivals and threshold events.

Typical board mode: hidden-world guide, route map or exhibition hub.

### Modern Heroic

Typical pressure: heroic identity, public image, private self, power expression, team chemistry, city or institutional crisis.

Typical entry point: icon roster, hero team, city, agency, base, villain gallery or crisis event.

Typical asset gravity: characters, teams, powers, factions, cities, events, equipment and signature locations.

Typical board mode: icon roster, franchise bible, faction/event map or creator workbench.

Modern Heroic is a primary family, not a sub-variant of mythic. It covers superhero and hero-roster worlds where recognizable character identity, power fantasy, team dynamics and contemporary commercial visual language are the main organizing logic.

### Military-Industrial

Typical pressure: command, logistics, doctrine, mobilization, production, attrition and front-line cost.

Typical entry point: theater, campaign, army, fleet, fortress, weapon system or chain of command.

Typical asset gravity: organizations, units, vehicles, weapons, locations, events and systems.

Typical board mode: campaign map, tactical brief or atlas desk.

Military-Industrial is usually a secondary lens. It should become primary only when the world is organized first by the war machine itself rather than by myth, politics, heroic identity or adventure.

## Diagnosis output

Each run should produce a short diagnosis before atom writing:

- primary family
- secondary family or lens
- narrative pressure
- entry point
- asset center of gravity
- relationship structure
- scale
- time structure
- visual recognition mechanism
- prose distance
- board reading mode
- implications for coverage, card writing, style and board layout

The diagnosis should be concise but decisive. It should explain what changes because of the judgment.

## Acceptance signals

A diagnosis passes when it explains why the world should not be built like a generic IP board.

It fails when it only repeats genre words.

It fails when primary and secondary families are treated equally and produce mixed signals.

It fails when the chosen family does not change coverage priority, card writing or board layout.

It fails when the diagnosis contradicts source evidence from Fandom, official material or the local wiki.
